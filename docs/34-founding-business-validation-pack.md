# Founding Business Validation Pack

## Purpose

This pack provides a repeatable, privacy-conscious way to test the OurValleys business proposition with local businesses before any real pilot, pricing commitment or public claim of validated demand.

It prepares the research materials only. No outreach may be sent in the product owner’s name without explicit approval. Findings must be reported as evidence, not converted into invented certainty.

## Research objectives

The interviews should establish:

1. Whether a free generated business website and local listing solves a meaningful problem.
2. Which parts of setup feel difficult, unclear or too time-consuming.
3. Whether businesses will maintain opening hours, services, contact details and media.
4. Which category-specific sections are genuinely useful.
5. What support businesses expect during onboarding and after launch.
6. Which optional paid outcomes have demonstrated demand, without presenting a fixed package or price.
7. Whether at least ten suitable businesses would consider a bounded private pilot.

## Sampling plan

Target 20–30 completed interviews across at least five categories. Aim for a useful mix of:

- trades;
- food and drink;
- retail;
- beauty and wellbeing;
- professional services;
- community organisations.

Include a spread of business maturity and current digital presence:

- no website;
- social-media-only presence;
- an outdated or difficult-to-maintain website;
- an actively maintained website;
- a home-based or service-area business that does not publish a street address.

Do not place private contact details, names, raw recordings or identifying interview notes in the public repository. Use participant codes such as `BIZ-001` in analysis records.

## Interview guide

### Opening

Explain that the conversation is research, not a sales call. State that OurValleys is exploring a free business website and local listing for RCT businesses. Ask permission to take structured notes and explain that published analysis will be anonymised.

### Current situation

1. How do customers currently find or contact your business?
2. What online profiles or websites do you currently maintain?
3. What works well and what causes frustration?
4. How much time do you normally spend updating business information?
5. What happens when opening hours, services or contact details change?
6. Do you pay for a website, directory listing, booking tool or advertising? Record bands rather than unnecessary exact financial details unless volunteered and relevant.

### Problem and outcome

7. What would a useful local business website need to achieve for you?
8. Which customer questions do you answer repeatedly?
9. What information is hardest to keep accurate across different platforms?
10. What would make a local platform feel trustworthy rather than like another directory?
11. What would make you stop using it?

### Proposition test

Present the proposition neutrally:

> OurValleys would let an eligible local business create a polished business website and local listing for free. The same structured information would power the standalone page, local search, category pages and town pages. The business could update its own details, services, opening hours, contact methods and media.

Then ask:

12. What is your immediate reaction?
13. Which part is most valuable, if any?
14. Which part is unclear or not credible?
15. Would you try it? Why or why not?
16. What would you need to see before trusting it with your business information?
17. Would you prefer to set it up yourself, receive assisted onboarding, or use a mixture?

### Onboarding and maintenance

18. Which information could you provide immediately?
19. Which information would take effort to prepare?
20. Would you be comfortable uploading a logo, photographs and service details?
21. How often would you realistically update the page?
22. Which reminders or support would help keep information current?
23. Are there details you would not want shown publicly, such as a home address?

### Category-specific needs

24. Which sections are essential for your type of business?
25. Which are unnecessary?
26. Test only relevant examples: services, price guidance, menus, treatments, appointment details, service areas, qualifications, facilities, accessibility information, offers or events.

### Optional paid outcomes

Do not lead with named packages or fixed pricing. Ask:

27. Are there outcomes beyond the free website that you currently pay for or might pay for?
28. Rank the usefulness of: enhanced design options, extra media, custom domain support, booking integrations, enquiry tools, promotional placement, analytics, assisted content setup and ongoing support.
29. Which of those would need to deliver a measurable result before you paid?
30. What would make a paid option feel unfair or damage trust in the free service?

### Pilot interest

31. Would you consider joining a small private pilot?
32. What would you expect from the pilot team?
33. What would prevent you from participating?
34. May the researcher contact you separately about a pilot? Store any contact permission outside the public repository.

### Close

Ask whether anything important was missed. Do not promise launch dates, permanent feature availability, pricing, traffic, verification status or commercial outcomes.

## Evidence coding framework

Code each material observation using one or more of:

- `DISCOVERY` — being found locally;
- `CREDIBILITY` — trust, presentation and legitimacy;
- `MAINTENANCE` — keeping information accurate;
- `SETUP` — onboarding effort or confusion;
- `PRIVACY` — public/private information boundaries;
- `MEDIA` — logo, photographs and asset quality;
- `CATEGORY` — category-specific content needs;
- `ENQUIRY` — contact and lead handling;
- `SUPPORT` — assisted setup or ongoing help;
- `ANALYTICS` — evidence of reach or outcomes;
- `PROMOTION` — paid or sponsored visibility;
- `INTEGRATION` — booking, domain or external tools;
- `TRUST_RISK` — scams, inaccurate data, lock-in or unfair monetisation;
- `PILOT` — willingness and conditions for participation.

For each theme, report:

- number of participants who raised it unprompted;
- number who agreed after prompting;
- category spread;
- strength of evidence;
- contradictory evidence;
- recommended product response.

Do not treat frequency alone as proof. Consider severity, affected business types and whether the observation reflects a real behaviour rather than a hypothetical preference.

## Paid-demand ranking

Rank optional outcomes only after interviews using:

| Score component | Range |
| --- | --- |
| Mentioned unprompted | 0–3 |
| Current spend or workaround exists | 0–3 |
| Clear measurable outcome described | 0–3 |
| Willingness to test | 0–2 |
| Broad category relevance | 0–2 |
| Trust or fairness risk | 0 to -3 |

Keep the free core promise intact. Paid status must not imply verification, ranking neutrality must be protected, and sponsored placement must be labelled.

## Approval-ready outreach wording

The following copy is prepared for review but must not be sent without authorisation:

> Hello, I’m helping research OurValleys, a proposed local platform for Rhondda Cynon Taf businesses and residents. We are exploring a free business website and local listing designed to be simple to create and maintain. This is research rather than a sales call, and nothing will be published without permission. Would you be willing to take part in a 25–35 minute conversation about how customers currently find your business and what would make a local digital service genuinely useful? Your feedback would be summarised anonymously, and there is no obligation to join a future pilot.

Follow-up wording:

> Thank you for considering the research. The discussion would cover your current website or social profiles, information-maintenance problems, the proposed free website and optional future features. We will not ask for passwords, financial account details or identity documents. Participation is voluntary and you can stop at any time.

## Representative example-page matrix

Create 5–10 fictional pages spanning different content shapes before moderated testing. Suggested examples:

1. Mobile plumbing and heating service with a hidden home address and service radius.
2. Independent café with menu highlights, opening exceptions and accessibility details.
3. Beauty practitioner with appointment-led services and price guidance.
4. Community organisation with recurring events and volunteer contact methods.
5. Professional service with qualifications, consultation areas and enquiry routing.
6. Retail shop with product categories, seasonal hours and an offer.
7. Long-name bilingual business using Welsh characters.
8. Image-light business that must still look credible with placeholders.

Clearly label all examples as fictional demonstrations.

## Synthesis report

After 20–30 interviews, publish an anonymised synthesis containing:

- sample composition;
- repeated problems and desired outcomes;
- proposition strengths and objections;
- onboarding effort median and range;
- maintenance willingness;
- category-specific differences;
- support expectations;
- pilot-interest count and conditions;
- ranked paid-outcome evidence;
- disproved assumptions;
- recommended changes to product documents;
- unresolved questions and research limitations.

Do not publish raw interview notes or information that could reasonably identify a participant.

## Decision rules

- Do not claim validated demand until the target sample and category spread are achieved.
- Do not commit to pricing from hypothetical willingness alone.
- Do not treat pilot interest as a binding commitment.
- Update product documentation when repeated evidence materially contradicts a central assumption.
- Retain configurable product choices where evidence remains mixed.
- Record owner approval before any external outreach is sent.
