# Founding Business Interview Guide

## Interview objective

Understand the business’s real current workflow and problems before presenting OurValleys. Do not begin by asking whether the person likes the idea; people are often polite about hypothetical products.

Target duration: 20–30 minutes.

## Opening

> Thank you for speaking with me. I am researching how local businesses in Rhondda Cynon Taf manage their website, business information and local visibility. This is product research rather than a sales call. I would like to understand what you currently do, what is difficult and whether a proposed local platform would be useful. I will only use the notes for developing and evaluating the project.

Confirm:

- Permission to take written notes.
- Separate explicit permission before recording audio or video.
- Whether comments may be quoted anonymously.

## Section 1 — Business context

1. Tell me briefly what the business does and where it serves.
2. How many people work in or help run it?
3. How do new customers usually find you?
4. Which customer enquiries matter most?
5. Are there times, locations or services you particularly want to promote?

Do not assume the owner wants more general traffic; identify the valuable customer action.

## Section 2 — Current digital presence

1. Do you currently have a website?
2. Who created it and who updates it?
3. What does it cost in money and time?
4. When was it last updated?
5. Which information becomes out of date most often?
6. Which other services do you maintain: Google, Facebook, Instagram, booking platforms, directories or marketplaces?
7. Where do you have to enter the same information more than once?
8. Have you lost enquiries or confused customers because information was missing or wrong?

Ask for a recent real example rather than a general opinion.

## Section 3 — Problems and workarounds

1. What is the most frustrating part of managing your online presence?
2. What do you avoid updating because it is too difficult or time-consuming?
3. What do customers repeatedly contact you to ask?
4. Have you paid for advertising or directory listings? What happened?
5. How do you know whether a website or advert has delivered value?
6. What would make you stop using a local business platform?

## Section 4 — Structured page exercise

Show or describe a realistic category-specific example page only after understanding the current workflow.

Ask the participant to work through the proposed fields:

- Business name and summary.
- Primary category.
- Location and areas served.
- Contact methods.
- Opening or availability hours.
- Services, products or menu.
- Price guidance.
- Images.
- Accessibility and Welsh-language service.
- Enquiry or booking route.
- Qualifications or evidence where relevant.

Observe:

- Fields they cannot answer.
- Information they consider private.
- Words they do not understand.
- Information that changes frequently.
- Information they would need help writing.
- Whether they can supply suitable photographs.

## Section 5 — Proposition test

Present the proposition neutrally:

> OurValleys would let a business enter its information once and receive a one-page website within a wider RCT discovery platform. The same information could appear in relevant search, town pages, events, offers and future tools. The basic page would be free, with optional paid tools later.

Ask:

1. What, if anything, would be useful about this?
2. What would make it not worth setting up?
3. What would you need to trust the platform?
4. Which information would you update yourself?
5. Would you prefer self-service, assisted setup or fully managed setup?
6. Would you be willing to complete a real pilot profile using your business information?
7. Who else would need to approve it?

Record actual commitment separately from verbal enthusiasm.

## Section 6 — Paid-value test

Do not ask only “Would you pay?” Present concrete capabilities one at a time.

Potential capabilities:

- Custom domain.
- Removal of platform branding.
- More pages or sections.
- Quote and booking tools.
- Products, menu or price catalogue.
- Enquiry management and follow-up.
- Advanced analytics.
- Multiple staff accounts.
- Offers and promotional tools.
- Managed setup or content assistance.

For each relevant capability ask:

1. What would this replace or improve?
2. How often would you use it?
3. What result would justify paying?
4. Would you trial it at a stated monthly price?
5. Which capability would you choose first if only one were available?

Use varied price points during the research plan; do not lead every participant towards the same answer.

## Section 7 — Closing

1. What have I not asked that would matter to a business like yours?
2. Which other local businesses should be included in the research?
3. May the project contact you about a private pilot?
4. What is your preferred contact route for that purpose?

Explain the next step accurately. Do not promise a launch date, traffic level, feature or permanent free premium service.

## Interviewer evidence summary

After the interview, record:

- Participant code and category.
- Current website state.
- Primary customer-acquisition route.
- Top three observed problems.
- Repeated-data burden.
- Most valuable proposed outcome.
- Structured onboarding difficulty.
- Trust concerns.
- Pilot commitment: none, interested, agreed, completed.
- Paid evidence: none, preference, trial intent, commitment.
- Estimated assisted setup minutes.
- Product assumptions supported or contradicted.
- Follow-up action and owner.

## Anti-bias rules

- Ask about recent behaviour before future intention.
- Do not defend the idea when criticised.
- Do not count a friend’s encouragement as independent validation.
- Record negative and neutral interviews.
- Separate a need for “more customers” from evidence that this product can deliver them.
- Do not imply that the platform is connected to RCT Council.
